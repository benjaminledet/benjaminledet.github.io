Chapter 1 · 2023 → June 2026

The migration: two apps, one codebase

In 2022, Yescapa ran two separate native apps, with every feature implemented twice and the iOS build taking two to three times the effort of the Android one. In 2023 I designed the way out. We would embed Flutter inside both native apps and migrate screen by screen, so the product roadmap never had to stop.

The Flutter architecture came from the Android rebuild I had led in 2020–2022 and from everything that running it in production had taught me; Android and Flutter remain very different, so it is the lessons that carried over, not the code.

Three calendar years separate that decision from the final release, and they overstate the effort. The early stretch was experimental, and I took the time to learn the framework properly. The dates say more about our constraints than about the size of the task.

The problem

Every feature was built twice

Two codebases meant two implementations and two QA passes for the same product, and the cost is visible in the release history.

Same product, same features to ship, and from 2020 to 2022 iOS released a third to a half as often as Android. That gap was the duplication tax users felt. Features were live on Android but still queued for iOS, while designs and wording drifted apart.

Technical debt aged at different speeds too. The iOS app had fallen behind, and with two codebases and everything built twice, that divergence was mechanical. Removing it for good was one of the main reasons we migrated, alongside the cost.

The chart also shows the migration working. From 2024, as Flutter gradually took over screens inside both apps, iOS release frequency went from 3.3 versions a month averaged over 2022–2023 to 6.5 over 2024–2025, because shipping iOS no longer meant rebuilding features in a second codebase.

A good share of those releases were fixes. I had taken over the iOS side to bring the app back up to standard, and we caught up on years of accumulated iOS bugs, plus the new ones created by the native-Flutter coexistence, which was complex to manage. Android slowed over the same window for a simpler reason. There were fewer bugs left to fix, so fewer fix releases to ship.

Since the June 2026 freeze, one implementation and one QA pass cover every platform and every brand at once.

Call the Android build of a feature one unit of work. Everything iOS added on top came to two or three more for us at the time, counting the second implementation, its QA round and the coordination between the two, so a simple feature came to 3–4 units. The same feature, built once on a shared codebase, costs 1.

The outcome

What the migration delivered, and what it cost

The migration had no budget of its own. The team in place delivered it in parallel with the full product roadmap, and the further it went, the cheaper it got, because each migrated screen meant new features were built once, in Flutter, freeing up time for the next step.

The real cost was elsewhere, and I’d rather say it plainly. The hybrid phase, with Flutter screens running inside the two native apps and depending on them for everything shared, was the most complex part of the project to manage, and the app lost some quality during that period. Migrating without stopping the roadmap is possible; it is not free.

The context helped too. In those years the mobile roadmap’s pace was set by other teams, which left us the slack to absorb the migration in parallel, and not every company has that slack.

The structural gain is permanent, because one codebase has nothing left to diverge from. The app is now the same on iOS and Android, same features and same screens, and more than 99.9% of its production sessions are crash-free.

The second brand came along almost for free. Every feature built for Yescapa lands in Goboony’s apps at the same time, on both stores, and no mobile team was hired for it.

The team

Half the team, four apps

The half-team figure needs a bit of context.

In 2022 every mobile feature was built twice, once for iOS and once for Android. Half of that team ships all four apps today. The developers who left did so by choice, and their positions were never backfilled, because one codebase serving every platform and both brands no longer needs the work done twice.

The developers who carried the migration all chose to become Flutter mobile developers, and made it official. Each of us already had some Flutter experience, which helped us make that choice, and we kept learning together, on the job, while shipping the roadmap.

I led that team through the whole migration, as their tech lead and manager. Migrating screen by screen was also how the developers trained on the architecture, and the ones who are staying now run it entirely on their own.

They work in the architecture I designed, on a codebase that carries none of the debt the two native apps had accumulated. The time that used to go into maintenance now goes into new features.

The bet

Why Flutter

Betting two production apps on a cross-platform framework is the kind of decision that can age badly, so it deserves its own scrutiny.

Cost was only part of what we were after. Two codebases meant features drifting apart, designs and wording diverging, two teams to keep synchronized, and years of technical debt on each side. Whatever we picked had to unify the teams and the features. The deployments too.

Flutter was a fit for us. It shares the whole app between platforms, UI included, so one implementation renders identically on iOS and Android, and divergence ends there.

Our app was a good candidate too, mostly data display and forms over an API. Heavier platform-specific needs would not have ruled Flutter out, since any native feature stays reachable through platform channels and native plugins, but having little of that kept our migration simple. Nothing in our toolchain had to be given up, since crash reporting, analytics, translations and automated releases all had first-class Flutter support.

The fair question in 2023 was whether Flutter would be another Cordova, one more hybrid wave that gets abandoned.

What convinced me at the time was that Google was converting its own apps to it, and reported 70% less engineering effort on Google Pay after the move. Big apps had already made the exact move we were planning. BMW rebuilt its diverging native apps as the single Flutter codebase behind My BMW; Nubank, the largest independent digital bank outside Asia, was migrating its app piece by piece; and SNCF Connect had replaced the separate native versions of France’s main rail app the year before. eBay, Toyota and Crédit Agricole had shipped on it too, and its popularity had grown every year in developer surveys.

Three years later the bet has aged well. Google announced at the end of 2024 that Flutter had passed one million monthly active developers, and that nearly 30% of all new iOS apps were built with it. Jetpack Compose and SwiftUI, the declarative frameworks the native platforms adopted themselves, are built on the same model, born on the web with React; Flutter bet on it from the start, and the native world moved the same way.

And the proof I can offer first-hand is on this page. Our app is in production on both stores, and it came out better than the native apps it replaced. We put everything we had learned on the native versions into it, and we used the migration to redesign the whole app for consistency.

None of that makes Flutter a universal answer. A mature native team with no duplication problem has little to gain from migrating. It was the right answer to our structure, where everything was built twice for a product that needed to be one app again.

Chapter 2 · July 2025 → July 2026

The AI year: my own throughput, ×4.4

With the platform converging, my own pace was the next thing to work on. I built a delivery system around Claude, inside the Flutter architecture I had designed: skills that describe how this codebase is built, commands for the recurring work, triggers and rules that enforce our standards, and automated releases, all versioned and reviewed like the rest of the code.

Then I measured what it changed against the only honest baseline I have, my own pre-AI pace, in the same codebase, counted by the same script.

These are my commits only, filtered by author in the repository’s history, so the other developers’ work is not in these numbers. And the ×4.4 compares the average pace of each era, 23 months before Claude against 12 months with it, not one month against another.

The test curve starts near zero because before AI I chose not to invest in automated tests. A company that wants to innovate fast doesn’t have time to linger on tests, and if you are rigorous enough to QA every case by hand, which takes a developer who knows their code well, tests become even more optional. Automated tests work best for developers who never try their code the way a user would. We shipped the occasional regression without them, but in my experience that cost us far less in production than the hours and hours of test-writing would have, and the app’s quality held.

Since January 2026 Claude writes tests alongside each feature, which is the 60K-line jump on the test curve. They cost me almost no time, so there was no reason not to. Months in, none of them has caught a regression yet; the bugs we did have got past them, because a test only checks what someone thought to check, and some bugs will always slip through. They remain an extra safety net that leaves the app stronger, at almost no cost, so I keep them. But I still believe efficient QA and real user testing beat unit tests. No other number on this page counts them.

The system

The config that keeps quality high

An AI without context writes plausible, generic code. Mine works inside an architecture I designed, through configuration written specifically for it, so every line the AI writes has a defined place in the system. That is what keeps the output at production quality.

13skills
It knows how this codebase works. The rules of the codebase, written down and loaded before the AI touches code: screen architecture, test-writing specs, the design-system catalog, native-code migration, crash triage.
12commands
Recurring work is one command. Prepare a change for review, generate tests, update the database schema, sync user journeys to Figma. Codified once, repeated identically.
6triggers & rules
Guardrails are enforced automatically. Triggers load the right skill on every request; hard rules ban the failure modes (off-brand UI components, unverified claims, “done” without checks).
5integrations
It sees beyond the code. Production crashes (Sentry), the app running on a device, the Figma designs, the Notion specs. The AI reads and drives them directly.
21scripts
Shipping is automated. Store releases, version checks, release notes, credentials. The path from merged code to the stores runs itself.

The whole configuration evolved through 96 commits over the year, written with Claude and reviewed like any other code. Every lesson learned becomes a written rule, so the system doesn’t make the same mistake twice. And it stays with the codebase, since new AI capability lands in the repository as configuration rather than tribal knowledge.

The developers who are staying built it with me through the year and are fully trained on it. How to use each part of it, and in which cases, is documented in Notion. None of it leaves with me.
